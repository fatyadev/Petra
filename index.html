Petra

Petra is an ancient city , the capital of Idumea (Edom), later the capital of the Nabataean Kingdom . The Nabataeans themselves called their city Rqm (Rakmu). Situated in what is now Jordan , at an altitude of over 900 m above sea level and 660 m above the surrounding terrain, in the Arava Valley , in the narrow Siq canyon .
The valley is entered through gorges located to the north and south, while to the east and west the cliffs drop off steeply, forming natural walls up to 60 m high. Not far from Petra are the Ad-Deir rock temple and Aaron's tomb .

Story

Petra was located at the crossroads of two important trade routes : one from the Red Sea to Damascus , the other from the Persian Gulf to Gaza on the Mediterranean coast . Caravans loaded with precious spices departing from the Persian Gulf had to brave the harsh conditions of the Arabian Desert for weeks until they reached the cool, narrow Siq canyon that led to the long-awaited Petra. There, travelers found food, shelter, and cool, life-giving water. Another major Nabatean center was Hegra .

For hundreds of years, trade brought great wealth to Petra. But when the Romans discovered sea routes to the East, the land trade in spices died out, and Petra gradually became deserted, lost in the sands. Many of Petra's structures were erected in different eras and under different rulers of the city, including the Idumeans ( 18th - 2nd centuries BC ), Nabataeans (2nd century BC - 106 AD ), Romans (106-395 AD ), Byzantines and Arabs . In the 12th century AD, they were owned by the Crusaders . The first modern European to see and describe Petra was the Swiss Johann Ludwig Burckhardt, who traveled incognito . Next to the ancient theater, you can see a building from the Idumean or Nabatean era. There are practically no monuments built after the 6th century AD, because by that time the city had already lost its significance. At the end of the 20th century, Petra became the most popular attraction in Jordan . In 2007, it was chosen as one of the new seven "wonders of the world" .
Today, about half a million tourists come to Jordan every year to see Petra, whose buildings bear witness to its glorious past. As tourists walk through the cool, kilometer-long Siq canyon, they turn a corner and come across Al Khazneh , a majestic building with a façade carved out of a huge rock. It is one of the best-preserved structures from the first century. The building is topped by a huge stone urn, which was said to contain gold and precious stones, hence the name of the temple (translated from Arabic as “treasury”). The canyon gradually widens, and tourists find themselves in a natural amphitheater, the sandstone walls of which are filled with caves. But the most striking thing is the crypts carved out of the rocks. The colonnade and amphitheater testify to the presence of the Romans in the city in the first and second centuries. Bedouins offer tired tourists camel rides , sell souvenirs and water their herds of goats at the city springs, whose waters quench the thirst of people and animals.

City of Petra

The territory of Petra occupies a large area. From the center, where the ruins of numerous buildings, no longer rocky, but built in the traditional way, from stone, are well preserved, it extends for several kilometers. The main street, stretching from east to west through the entire city, was laid out during the Roman rule. On both sides of it there is a majestic colonnade. The western end of the street abutted a large temple, and the eastern one ended with a three-span triumphal arch. Ad-Deir , a monastery carved into the rock on the top of a cliff, is a huge building about 50 m wide and over 45 m high. Judging by the crosses carved on the walls, the temple served as a Christian church for some time. Later, after researchers excavated the area beneath the monastery, they discovered the tomb of one of the Nabataean kings.

Having mastered the art of collecting water, the inhabitants of Petra also mastered the art of working with stone. The very name "Petra" means " stone " in Greek ( Greek: πετρα ). And Petra was indeed a city of stone, the likes of which were not found in the Roman Empire. The Nabataeans, who built the city, patiently carved houses, crypts and temples from stone blocks. Petra was nestled among the red sandstones, which are excellent for construction, and by the first century AD, a monumental city had grown up in the heart of the desert. With the help of terracotta pipes, the architects of Petra created a complex water supply system and, despite the arid climate, the city's inhabitants never needed water. Around 200 reservoirs were located throughout the city, collecting and storing rainwater. In addition to connecting the reservoirs, terracotta pipes collected water from all sources within a radius of 25 kilometers. The architects planned the construction of the famous temple-mausoleum Al-Khazneh in the former riverbed. To build this structure, the riverbed was changed, a grandiose project for that time. A tunnel was cut into the rock, diverting the flow of water and a series of dams were built.
The famous rock temple - mausoleum , "Pharaoh's Treasury", as the Arabs call it. The exact purpose of the structure is not fully understood. There is an assumption that it was originally a temple to the goddess Isis . In any case, many features of the monument indicate that it could have been built by craftsmen familiar with the architectural techniques of Alexandria, Egypt . The huge surface of the rock was cut down. But for this it was necessary to erect scaffolding , and there are almost no trees in this area. In the absence of scaffolding, it was possible, without cutting down the entire surface at once, to take advantage of the unevenness of the rock and walk along it like steps. In this case, both the stonemason and the carver would start from the very top, cutting out the first step, and then go lower and lower. After all, it is one thing to mark out a future building and cut it out while standing on scaffolding, and quite another to do the same while hanging over an abyss.
